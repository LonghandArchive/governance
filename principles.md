# Operating principles

The Longhand Archive is a personal project, built in public, that archives UK public sector data and derives intelligence from it. These principles describe how the project operates. They are not aspirations — they are the rules the project holds itself to.

**Archival integrity over convenience.** The archive is the authoritative record. Everything built on top of it — databases, websites, APIs — is a derived view, rebuildable from the archive. When archival integrity and operational convenience conflict, integrity wins.

**Preservation serves intelligence.** The archive exists to be understood, not just kept. The project derives analytical views that surface patterns, trends, and insight that would not be visible from a snapshot of the present alone. Preservation without analysis is storage; analysis without preservation is opinion.

**Deliberate pace.** Structure is chosen before code is written. Decisions are recorded before they are implemented. The project optimises for the quality of what endures, not the speed at which things appear.

**Built in public.** The project is a personal learning log as much as a data artefact. Decisions, reasoning, and reversals are visible. The messiness is part of the value; the polish is not the point.

**The process is the product.** The project is a practical demonstration of how one person builds a substantive system using AI-assisted tools. The architecture, the decisions, and the working method are documented as they happen. The reasoning is intended to be as reusable as the code.

**Independent and non-endorsed.** The project is not affiliated with or endorsed by any UK government department or any other organisation. It is a personal undertaking. It uses public data under the Open Government Licence. Its analytical posture is observational, not adversarial.

**Privacy by design.** Personal data is minimised at the earliest possible boundary. The public surface never reveals more than its purpose requires.

**Generalisation by evidence.** Abstractions are extracted from at least two concrete examples, not guessed up front. The first instance of anything is allowed to be specific. Shared patterns are proven, not assumed.
